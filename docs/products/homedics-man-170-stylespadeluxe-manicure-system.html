First of all, we had a slightly different model, the MAN-200, but based on several other reviewer's comments, I think that some have had the same experience as we did with ours, so I'm posting the review here. The different models share many features, so this review is pertinent as to most particulars. I'm including some tips garnered from long usage of these. Ours worked fine for several years, until I eventually overtaxed it by running it too long-working on some very thick toenails. The charging cord plug end that attaches to the handpiece is a weak point in the basic design, as is the charger. The motor is not a strong one but it's adequate. It will easily stop if too much pressure is used. If you want a faster removal of material, other machines are out there. Overall I'd give this a B+ for what it claims to do vs what it does. 4.5 out of 5 stars, just don't expect the performance of a professional/commercial version with which you may have had first hand experience. A very light touch is all that's necessary (or adviseable) for the HoMedics bits to work properly in most cases. Working in one spot too long or using excessive pressure can produce significant frictional heat-especially objectionable if working on the nail surface directly above the quick itself, and quite possibly actual lasting pain if you're not careful, so be forewarned. Be especially careful about reducing nail thickness. If you've used yours for a while and you encounter a situation where more working pressure or time is required than usual to do what you've done before, the abrasive part of the bit is most likely partially clogged. (When that happens the abrasive acts as if it's a finer grade and cuts more slowly due to the reduced depth between the grains of abrasive.) The abrasive itself is much harder and tougher than human (or dog) nails-but it is susceptible to loading up. Depending on what it's clogged with will indicate what to use to try to clean it off/out. Nail polish may require a solvent to remove from the abrasive effectively, and if so, be careful to avoid getting any solvent on the plastic parts. A hard bristle toothbrush or wire brush and water should work to remove natural nail residue from the abrasive-maybe add a little scouring powder if that doesn't get it all off. Working on fully dry nails that do not have any foreign coating on them creates a dry dust that does not stick to the bits much at all. Wet, polished or nails with any softenable coating will have a greater tendency to coat the abrasive and reduce it's effectiveness. The metal bit ends have a fused-on coating that will NOT come off off as would the abrasive on an emery board-effectively lasting the life of the machine when used only on nails. On the other hand, because the abrasive is so tough, it can reduce the THICKNESS as well as the length of nails wherever it is used, so be careful when working around the cuticle as you can easily and QUICKLY thin the nail so much that it will cause a problem-as another reviewer mentioned. The method used to secure the bits/attachments is adequate, but it's definitely not the most positive bit-retention system I've ever come across, and under certain circunmstances the bits are likely to work loose. When that happens they don't fly away, so it's not a big deal. Don't attempt to jam them back into the handpiece while it's running-you'll eventually damage the meshing parts if you do that. If a bit doesn't seem to \\"snap\\" in easily, rotate the bit's working tip (the turning part, not the base of the bit) relative to the handpiece until it does. You can easily see the orientation required of the bit to mesh properly by simply looking into the handpiece's bit recess and into the base of the bit, and manualy aligning the bit visually if you come across an occasional difficult matchup. THE MOST IMPORTANT THING TO DO WHEN YOU GET ONE OF THESE IS TO read THE MANUAL COVER TO COVER-IT'S ONLY A FEW PAGES PEOPLE! If you don't read the manual there are things you may find objectionable, and which may cause you to make complaints, but which are clearly stated. In this model's manual, for those impatient people, it clearly says to charge it for 16 hours the first time. HOWEVER, what it ALSO says is that this model can be used WITH the power supply cord attached. (The MAN-200 model and some others can ONLY be used with the power supply attached to the handle, but aren't rechargeable.....) So, in effect, you have 2 operating modes with this model: 1. cordless after being charged, and 2. With the cord attached if it's inconvenient for you to charge it fully between uses. It also say to NOT use the device for more than 20 minutes continuously. BELIEVE THIS. That's about how long it will take the CHARGING UNIT to heat up to the self-destruct point if it's being run while plugged into the power supply. I know this because this is how I killed our power supply (MAN-200)-by overheating it. Afterwards the handpiece will still be capable of working, but if you can't recharge or power it, it's useless. The charger alone will be exorbitantly expensive to replace from HoMedics, and you'll spend mor etime hunting one down elsewhere than it's worth..... You can also overheat the power supply in LESS than 20 minutes (or run the battery down faster) by using more pressure, so keep the pressure light and if anything STARTS TO FEEL MORE THAN WARM (power supply or handpiece or your nail), take a break to let it cool off. The power supply in several HoMedics units INCLUDING THIS ONE are constructed in such a way that they cannot cope with overheating except by self-destructing. Better power supplies have an internal thermal overload cut-out feature so they can reset themselves when they cool down, but it costs more to make a power supply with this feature. The power supply could be simply redesigned with a vented case and/or a heat sink to help with passive cooling, but eventhis obviously costs more than what HoMedics feels is warranted, given the selling prce of these manicure sets, so from that standpoint it's understandable for corners to be cut in their design. It's cheaper to print a \\"20 minute\\" warning in the manual than to do anything else. A few things that are not mentioned in the manual should be kept in mind with any NiCad or NiMH battery equipped rechargeable unit. In general, NiCad-equipped devices should not be KEPT attached to the plugged-in charger all the time for days or weeks on end as this can kill the charger/power supply and/or battery. (NiMH batteries hold a charge much longer than NiCad's once charged so they don't NEED to be recharged when disused for weeks or a couple months-I suspect these are NiCads-they're cheaper to manufacture.) No matter which battery is in the unit, NiCad AND NiMH battery-equipped devices will both often increase their usable capacity (running time) after being cycled (charged and discharged) several times, so if the run time is very short the first time out, don't necessarily give up, it will PROBABLY get better. IF it doesn't that's what a warranty is for. In fact, I'd reccomend just turning the unit on and letting it run with no load after the first recharge to get an idea of how long it will run, then recharge it and use it. Remember though that it's adviseable to NOT discharge the battery until it's COMPLETELY DEAD before recharging it as this will sometimes decrease the battery's actual ability to be recharged or may discharge it to the point that the charger will THINK that the battery is dead, because the voltage is lower that the designed-in point at which the charger will allow it to be charged even if the charger is ok. So, when it slows down a fair amount, or when it stops more easily under a light load, turn it off, put it aside to let it cool down, then recharge it.
